Our Galaxy: The Milky Way

The Milky Way appeared to the ancients as a diffuse band of light across the night sky. With the use of a telescope Galileo was able to see that this band of light was actually made up of a great concentration of stars. Now we know that the Milky Way is a galaxy that we view from the inside. The Milky Way (or the galaxy) has a large central bulge containing the nucleus, a flattened disk, and is surrounded by halo of stars and globular clusters. There are around 400 billion stars in the galaxy, and a total luminosity equivalent to 20 billion Suns. The galaxy is some 30 kpc across, with a central bulge around 10 kpc in diameter. The Solar System orbits the centre of the galaxy, at a distance of about 8 kpc, completing an orbit every 250 million years, and travelling at approximately 220 km/s. The galaxy is around 13 billion years old. Light takes 100,000 years to travel across the galaxy from one edge to the other.


History of our understanding of our Galaxy

The first attempt to locate the Sun’s position in the Galaxy was conducted by Jacobus Cornelius Kapteyn in the early 1900s. Based on the density of stars around the Sun, and seeing how rapidly the number of stars increases with decreasing brightness (implying distance), Kapteyn observed that the density of stars appeared to fall off in all directions of the Sun. He concluded that we were at the densest part of the galaxy and therefore must be at the centre, but his method was flawed. More precise distance determinations were possible by studying Cepheids. Cepheids are variable stars whose inherent brightness is proportional to the period of their variability. By measuring the period of their variability we can determine their true brightness, and then from their apparent brightness we can measure their distance from us. This has allowed astronomers to measure the distances to such stars and refine our model for the structure of the galaxy. In 1917 Harlow Shapley studied the distribution of globular clusters in the night sky and their locations relative to the Sun and the disk of the Milky Way. He observed that the globular clusters are arranged in a spherical volume centred on a point several thousand parsecs from the Sun. He argued that this point was the centre of the galaxy. Jan Oort and Bertil Lindblad studied the motion of stars near the Sun. They found that the Sun and nearby stars orbited a distant point in the direction that Harley Shapley had argued as the centre of the galaxy. Stars 28 “closer in” orbit faster, while those further away orbit slower. This supported Shapley’s view. Robert Trumpler in 1930 found that the Galaxy is filled with an interstellar haze that makes stars appear fainter than they would otherwise appear. He learnt this by studying the apparent brightnesses of star clusters, whose distances were known. Distant clusters were fainter than they should be and the effect was found to increase with increasing distance. This is the reason Kapteyn’s results were wrong. This is the Interstellar Medium (ISM). Until then we had only known about the dense clouds and nebulae, but now we knew that the entire space between the stars was filled with diffuse gas and dust
